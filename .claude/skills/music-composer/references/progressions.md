# Common Chord Progressions

## Pop & Rock

### I-V-vi-IV (The "Axis" Progression)
Keys: Any major
Example in C: C - G - Am - F
Used in: Countless pop hits ("Let It Be", "Don't Stop Believin'", "With or Without You")
Character: Uplifting, anthemic, resolves well

### I-IV-V (Classic Rock)
Keys: Any major
Example in C: C - F - G
Used in: Rock & roll, blues rock, country
Character: Strong, traditional, resolves powerfully

### vi-IV-I-V (Emotional Pop)
Keys: Any major
Example in C: Am - F - C - G
Used in: Emotional pop, singer-songwriter
Character: Starts minor, moves to resolution

### I-bVII-IV (Rock Shuffle)
Keys: Any major
Example in C: C - Bb - F
Used in: Classic rock, hard rock
Character: Bluesy, rebellious, mixolydian sound

### i-bVII-bVI-bVII (Andalusian)
Keys: Any minor
Example in Am: Am - G - F - G
Used in: Rock, metal, flamenco
Character: Dark, dramatic, descending

## Blues

### 12-Bar Blues (Standard)
Structure: I-I-I-I-IV-IV-I-I-V-IV-I-V
Example in A: A7-A7-A7-A7-D7-D7-A7-A7-E7-D7-A7-E7
Used in: Blues, rock, jazz
Character: Classic blues feel, turnaround at end

### 8-Bar Blues
Structure: I-IV-I-V-IV-I-V-I
Shorter form, common in early blues

### Minor Blues
Structure: i7-i7-i7-i7-iv7-iv7-i7-i7-V7-iv7-i7-V7
Example in Am: Am7-Am7-Am7-Am7-Dm7-Dm7-Am7-Am7-E7-Dm7-Am7-E7

## Jazz

### ii-V-I (The Foundation)
Keys: Any major
Example in C: Dm7 - G7 - Cmaj7
Used in: Jazz standards, bossa nova
Character: Strong resolution, endless variations possible

### I-vi-ii-V (Rhythm Changes, "I Got Rhythm")
Keys: Any major
Example in C: Cmaj7 - Am7 - Dm7 - G7
Used in: Jazz standards, swing
Character: Circular, strong harmonic movement

### iii-VI-ii-V (Minor ii-V)
Keys: Any major
Example in C: Em7 - A7 - Dm7 - G7
Used in: Jazz, bridge sections
Character: More complex, chromatic movement

### Jazz Blues
Structure: I7-IV7-I7-I7-IV7-#ivdim7-I7-VI7-ii7-V7-I7-V7
More sophisticated than standard blues

### Giant Steps (Coltrane Changes)
Complex progression moving through three keys
Pattern: I-bIII-bVI (repeat in different keys)
Character: Challenging, modern jazz

## Classical

### I-IV-I-V-I (Authentic Cadence)
Universal classical progression
Strong resolution

### I-IV-I (Plagal Cadence - "Amen")
Keys: Any major
Example in C: C - F - C
Character: Gentle, religious, final

### I-vi-IV-V (50s Progression)
Keys: Any major
Example in C: C - Am - F - G
Used in: Doo-wop, early rock & roll
Character: Nostalgic, vintage

### Circle of Fifths Progression
Pattern: I-IV-vii°-iii-vi-ii-V-I
Example in C: C-F-Bdim-Em-Am-Dm-G-C
Character: Smooth voice leading, classical

## Modal Progressions

### Dorian Vamp
Pattern: i-IV
Example in D Dorian: Dm - G
Used in: Jazz, funk, modal jazz
Character: Static but groovy, no resolution needed

### Mixolydian
Pattern: I-bVII
Example in G Mixolydian: G - F
Used in: Rock, folk, Celtic
Character: Major with flat 7, rock feel

### Phrygian
Pattern: i-bII
Example in E Phrygian: Em - F
Used in: Metal, flamenco, Spanish music
Character: Dark, exotic, tense

## Electronic & EDM

### One-Chord Vamp
Pattern: Single chord (often minor)
Example: Am (throughout)
Used in: Techno, minimal, ambient
Character: Hypnotic, based on rhythm/texture

### i-VI-III-VII (Minor EDM)
Keys: Any minor
Example in Am: Am - F - C - G
Used in: Progressive house, trance
Character: Emotional, building

### I-V-vi-iii-IV-I-IV-V (8-bar EDM)
Extended progression for build-ups
Character: Expansive, euphoric

## Latin

### Bossa Nova
Pattern: Imaj7-vi7-ii7-V7
Example in C: Cmaj7-Am7-Dm7-G7
Character: Smooth, jazzy, Brazilian

### Salsa/Son Montuno
Pattern: Often two-chord vamp (I-IV or I-V)
Based more on rhythm than harmony
Character: Dance-oriented, percussive

## Film & Cinematic

### Emotional/Epic
Pattern: vi-IV-I-V or I-V-vi-IV
Often with strings and orchestration
Character: Sweeping, emotional

### Suspense
Pattern: i-bII (Phrygian) or chromatic movement
Character: Tense, uncertain

### Hero Theme
Pattern: I-V-vi-IV or I-IV-V-I
Major key, strong resolution
Character: Triumphant, inspiring

## Special Techniques

### Parallel Harmony
Moving chord qualities in parallel motion
Example: Cmaj-Dmaj-Emaj (not diatonic)
Used in: Impressionist, modern pop
Character: Colorful, floating

### Pedal Point
Sustained bass note while harmony changes above
Example: Bass on C while chords change: C-F/C-G/C
Character: Drone-like, hypnotic

### Secondary Dominants
V7 of any chord
Example in C: D7-G (D7 is V7/V)
Character: Extra tension and color

### Modal Interchange (Borrowed Chords)
Borrowing chords from parallel key
Example in C major: Fm (from C minor)
Character: Unexpected color, emotional shift

## Substitutions

### Tritone Substitution (Jazz)
Replace V7 with bII7
Example: Db7 instead of G7 (in key of C)
Character: Chromatic, sophisticated

### Relative Substitution
Replace major with relative minor (or vice versa)
Example: Am instead of C
Character: Subtle color change

## Tips for Writing Progressions

1. **Start Simple**: I-IV-V-I or I-V-vi-IV work everywhere
2. **Add Color**: Replace major with maj7, minor with m7
3. **Create Movement**: Use ii-V-I for strong resolution
4. **Avoid Clichés**: Vary common progressions slightly
5. **Match Genre**: Blues needs dominant 7ths, pop often uses triads
6. **Voice Leading**: Move voices smoothly between chords
7. **Experiment**: Try modes, borrowed chords, unusual resolutions
