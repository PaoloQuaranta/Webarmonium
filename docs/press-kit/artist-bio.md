## 300-word bio

> Paolo Quaranta (b. 1968, Viareggio; lives and works in Varese, Italy) is a self-taught Italian artist working between generative systems, networked instruments, and sound. His practice asks how musical and visual authorship reorganize themselves when the maker is not a single hand but a network of human activity, autonomous algorithms, and protocols.
>
> A classically trained flutist, vocalist in progressive rock bands, and builder of analog modular synthesizers, Quaranta has spent decades moving between performance, hand-built electronic instruments, and code. He works as an educator with people with disabilities, a long-running practice that informs his attention to gesture, accessibility, and the conditions under which authorship is shared rather than solitary.
>
> Between 2022 and 2024, working under the pseudonym "OldFart," he released a body of more than forty generative works across Tezos (objkt.com), Ethereum (foundation.app, manifold), and other on-chain platforms. The works centre on digital freehand and generative art—the negotiation between embodied gesture and algorithmic process. Notable individual pieces include *The Summoning* (2022, Tezos) and *Escape Your Skin* (2023, Ethereum/Manifold).
>
> *Webarmonium* (2026) marks his first artwork released under his own name and his first persistent, browser-based net-art instrument. It is a real-time deterministic sonification of three open public data streams—Wikipedia Recent Changes, HackerNews new stories, GitHub Public Events—paired with a multi-user collaborative composition layer that allows up to four remote performers to play together over a shared browser session. The work was developed in iterative dialogue with a large language model and exists only and continuously at webarmonium.net—not packaged, not downloadable, not installable. The source code is open on GitHub.
>
> *Webarmonium* synthesizes the threads of his earlier practice: the breath of the flute, the layered voicings of prog rock, the patch logic of modular synthesis, and the algorithmic gesture of generative art—now distributed across an entire network of strangers.
