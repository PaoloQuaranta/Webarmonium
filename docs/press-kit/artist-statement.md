# Artist Statements — Webarmonium

The full set of three drafted statements lives at [`/docs/marketing/artist-statements-2026.md`](../marketing/artist-statements-2026.md). They are reproduced here so the press kit is self-contained.

Three variants by register. Use the appropriate one per channel.

---

## Variant A — Concept-forward

**Use for:** Rhizome / Artbase, Creative Applications, Net Art Anthology, We Make Money Not Art, Neural.it.

> Webarmonium is a musical instrument that plays the web.
>
> A traditional instrument translates a single performer's gesture into sound. Webarmonium replaces that gesture with two forms of distributed interaction. On the landing page, three real-time public data streams—Wikipedia edits, HackerNews posts, GitHub commits—become notes: position determines pitch, source determines timbre. The composition emerges from the unconscious activity of millions who will never hear it.
>
> In collaborative rooms, up to four remote performers compose together through layered tools: gestural input (tap, hold, drag), programmable step sequencers, three fully synthesized drum kits, and a sound-design panel for shaping each voice. An audition mode generates phrases autonomously using golden-ratio interval timing, so the instrument continues to perform itself when no human touches it.
>
> The mapping is deterministic. There is no randomness, only the structured translation of human activity—conscious and unconscious, programmatic and gestural—into sound. What we call musical authorship is here distributed across data streams, anonymous strangers, autonomous algorithms, and the few performers who happen to share a browser session at any given moment.
>
> Webarmonium asks what becomes of authorship when the score is written, in real time, by everyone, no one, and the instrument itself.
>
> Paolo Quaranta, 2026. Live at webarmonium.net.

## Variant B — Process / system-forward

**Use for:** Ars Electronica Prix, Sonar+D, MUTEK, transmediale, festival catalogues, Lumen Prize, STARTS Prize.

> Webarmonium is a deterministic real-time sonification system that operates on three open public data streams—Wikipedia Recent Changes, HackerNews new stories, and GitHub Public Events—and on the gestures and compositional inputs of remote co-performers connected through shared browser sessions.
>
> Each network event is translated into a note through a fixed mapping: horizontal position determines frequency in a 220–880 Hz band; vertical position modulates harmonic content and amplitude; data source selects timbre. The same input always yields the same output.
>
> Collaborative rooms expose a layered authoring stack to up to four performers: gestural input (tap, hold, drag); a step sequencer of 3–16 steps for melodic or rhythmic patterns; three fully synthesized drum kits (808, Acoustic, Electronic) with no sample files; a sound-design panel of eight synthesizer presets and per-voice controls; and an audition mode that generates phrases autonomously using PHI-based interval timing. All inputs feed an algorithmic composition engine modeling form, voice leading, and twenty genre profiles. The room maintains a 24-hour rolling memory of interaction patterns that shapes its tonal character over time.
>
> The work runs in vanilla JavaScript with Tone.js, Socket.io, and PixiJS, developed entirely through iterative dialogue with a large language model. It exists only at webarmonium.net.
>
> Paolo Quaranta, 2026.

## Variant C — Lyrical

**Use for:** e-flux announcements, The Creative Independent, longform interviews, press features.

> Somewhere right now, an editor is correcting a typo on the Wikipedia page for *Napoleon*. A developer in Bangalore is pushing a commit. A teenager in Brooklyn is upvoting a story about chip fabrication. None of them know each other, none of them are listening. Webarmonium hears them anyway.
>
> The work is a musical instrument that takes the public surface of the internet—the parts where humans leave traces in real time—and translates each trace into a note. The gesture of the performer, that most cherished category of musical practice, is here distributed across millions of people who never agreed to perform.
>
> A second mode opens collaborative rooms where remote participants meet to play the instrument intentionally—through gestures, but also through sequencers they program, drum kits they shape, synthesizer voices they sculpt. An autonomous mode keeps the instrument singing when no one is touching it, generating phrases on the timing of the golden ratio.
>
> They do not see each other's faces. They hear each other's hands. They hear, also, what their hands set in motion when they walk away.
>
> Webarmonium is interested in what music sounds like when authorship dissolves into network. It is also interested in the moment, after authorship dissolves, when two people in a room reach toward each other anyway.
>
> Paolo Quaranta, 2026. Live and continuous at webarmonium.net.
